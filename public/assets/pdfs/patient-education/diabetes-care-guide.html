Understanding Diabetes

Diabetes is a group of diseases that result in high blood sugar (glucose) levels. When you have diabetes, your body either doesn't make enough insulin or can't effectively use the insulin it makes. Over time, high blood sugar can cause serious health complications.

Types of Diabetes

Type 1 Diabetes

  • Usually diagnosed in children and young adults
  • Body's immune system attacks insulin-producing cells
  • Requires daily insulin injections
  • Accounts for about 5-10% of all diabetes cases
  • Cannot be prevented

Type 2 Diabetes

  • Most common form (90-95% of cases)
  • Usually develops in adults over 45, but increasingly seen in younger people
  • Body becomes resistant to insulin or doesn't produce enough
  • Often manageable with lifestyle changes and medications
  • Can often be prevented or delayed

Gestational Diabetes

  • Develops during pregnancy
  • Usually resolves after childbirth
  • Increases risk for Type 2 diabetes later in life
  • Can affect baby's health if not managed properly

Prediabetes

  • Blood sugar higher than normal but not high enough for diabetes diagnosis
  • Often no symptoms
  • Strong risk factor for developing Type 2 diabetes
  • Can be reversed with lifestyle changes

Signs and Symptoms

Common Symptoms

  • Increased thirst and urination: Body tries to flush out excess glucose
  • Unexplained weight loss: Body breaks down muscle and fat for energy
  • Fatigue: Cells can't get enough glucose for energy
  • Blurred vision: High blood sugar affects the lens of the eye
  • Slow-healing wounds: High glucose impairs healing
  • Frequent infections: High blood sugar weakens immune system
  • Numbness or tingling: In hands or feet (nerve damage)

Risk Factors

Type 2 Diabetes Risk Factors

  • Age: 45 years or older
  • Weight: Being overweight or obese
  • Family history: Parent or sibling with diabetes
  • Physical inactivity: Less than 3 times per week
  • Race/ethnicity: African American, Hispanic, Native American, Asian American, Pacific Islander
  • High blood pressure: 140/90 or higher
  • Abnormal cholesterol: HDL under 35 or triglycerides over 250
  • Gestational diabetes history: Previous gestational diabetes or baby over 9 pounds
  • PCOS: Polycystic ovary syndrome

Diabetes Management

Blood Sugar Monitoring

üìä TARGET BLOOD SUGAR LEVELS:
  • Before meals: 80-130 mg/dL
  • 2 hours after meals: Under 180 mg/dL
  • A1C goal: Under 7% for most adults
  • Bedtime: 100-140 mg/dL

Your doctor may set different targets based on your individual needs.

How to Check Blood Sugar

  1. Wash hands with soap and warm water
  2. Insert test strip into glucose meter
  3. Use lancing device to prick side of fingertip
  4. Touch drop of blood to test strip
  5. Read result and record in logbook
  6. Dispose of lancet and test strip safely

When to Check Blood Sugar

  • Before meals
  • 2 hours after meals
  • Before bedtime
  • Before and after exercise
  • When feeling symptoms of high or low blood sugar
  • During illness

Foods to Choose More Often

  • Non-starchy vegetables: Fresh, frozen, or canned without added salt
  • Fruits: Fresh, frozen, or canned in water or own juice
  • Whole grains: Brown rice, quinoa, oats, whole wheat bread
  • Lean proteins: Fish, poultry, eggs, beans, nuts, tofu
  • Low-fat dairy: Milk, yogurt, cheese
  • Healthy fats: Avocado, olive oil, nuts, seeds

Foods to Limit

  • Sugary drinks (soda, fruit punch, sweet tea)
  • Processed and packaged foods
  • White bread, white rice, regular pasta
  • Fried foods
  • Foods high in saturated and trans fats
  • Foods high in sodium
  • Sweets and desserts

Carbohydrate Counting

Carbohydrates have the biggest impact on blood sugar. Learn to count carbs to help manage blood sugar levels:

  • 1 carb serving = 15 grams of carbs
  • Most adults need 3-4 carb servings per meal
  • Read nutrition labels carefully
  • Measure and weigh foods until you can estimate portions
  • Keep a food diary to track patterns

Physical Activity

Benefits of Exercise for Diabetes

  • Lowers blood sugar immediately and over time
  • Improves insulin sensitivity
  • Helps with weight management
  • Reduces cardiovascular risk
  • Improves mood and energy levels
  • Strengthens bones and muscles

Exercise Recommendations

  • Aerobic exercise: At least 150 minutes of moderate intensity weekly
  • Resistance training: 2 or more days per week
  • Flexibility exercises: 2-3 days per week
  • Daily activity: Avoid sitting for more than 30 minutes at a time
‚ö†Ô∏è EXERCISE SAFETY TIPS:
  • Check blood sugar before, during, and after exercise
  • Carry fast-acting carbs (glucose tablets, juice)
  • Stay hydrated
  • Check feet daily for sores or injuries
  • Wear proper footwear
  • Exercise with a buddy when possible

Medications

Type 1 Diabetes Medications

  • Insulin: Required for survival, delivered by injection or pump
  • Types: Rapid-acting, short-acting, intermediate-acting, long-acting

Type 2 Diabetes Medications

  • Metformin: Usually first medication prescribed
  • Sulfonylureas: Help pancreas release more insulin
  • DPP-4 inhibitors: Help lower blood sugar
  • GLP-1 agonists: Slow digestion, help with weight loss
  • SGLT-2 inhibitors: Help kidneys remove glucose through urine
  • Insulin: May be needed as diabetes progresses
üíä MEDICATION TIPS:
  • Take medications exactly as prescribed
  • Don't skip doses
  • Keep medications in original containers
  • Set reminders if you forget doses
  • Tell your doctor about all medications and supplements
  • Know what to do if you miss a dose

Preventing Complications

Regular Health Screenings

  • A1C test: Every 3-6 months
  • Blood pressure: At every appointment
  • Cholesterol: At least once yearly
  • Eye exam: Annually with an eye doctor
  • Foot exam: At every appointment, daily self-checks
  • Kidney function: At least annually
  • Dental checkup: Every 6 months

Daily Self-Care

  • Check feet daily for cuts, sores, or changes
  • Take care of teeth and gums
  • Manage stress
  • Don't smoke
  • Get adequate sleep
  • Stay up to date on vaccinations

Managing Low Blood Sugar (Hypoglycemia)

Symptoms of Low Blood Sugar (Under 70 mg/dL)

  • Shaking or trembling
  • Sweating
  • Rapid heartbeat
  • Dizziness
  • Hunger
  • Confusion
  • Irritability

Treatment: 15-15 Rule

  1. Eat 15 grams of fast-acting carbs:
    • 3-4 glucose tablets
    • ¬Ω cup fruit juice
    • 1 tablespoon honey
    • 5-6 hard candies
  2. Wait 15 minutes
  3. Check blood sugar again
  4. If still under 70 mg/dL, repeat treatment
  5. Once blood sugar is normal, eat a meal or snack
